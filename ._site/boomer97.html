The Associated Press 

The big blast and tremble residentshere reported this week wasn't an earthquake but probably a sonic boomcaused by an aircraft breaking the sound barrier, officials say. 

And the earthquake like experience mayreturn this week when the plane returns. The loud noise that reportedlyshook the ground Thursday was caused by an SR71 Blackbird spy plane travelingacross the country for a demonstration, said Sally Freis, a spokeswomanfor Offutt Air Force Base in Nebraska. 

The plane was traveling from Beale AirForce Base in California en route to Andrews Air Force Base in Maryland.Lt. Chris Karns, another spokesman for Offutt, said the aircraft is expectedto return following the air show perhaps on Monday. "The aircraft wouldhave been in (northwest Missouri) about 2:30 or 3 in the afternoon (Thursday),"said Freis. "We were directed to tell people that if they heard somethingit was a sonic boom caused by the aircraft." 

The Offutt public affairs office handlesreports on the effects of sonic booms, Karns said. So far, Offutt has receiveda few calls from Missouri and Kentucky, Freis said. An SR71 Blackbird seta crosscountry record in March 1990 by flying from Oxnard, Calif., to Salisbury,Md., in 68 minutes, 17 seconds. 

Residents in and around the St. Josepharea reported hearing a loud noise and feeling the earth tremble about2:30 p.m. Thursday. Some earthquake specialists even reported slight readingson richter scales. No injuries or damages were reported. 

St. Joseph police officer Archie Auxiersaid the boom felt first like someone hit his house, and then things beganto shake. 

Beverly Dunn, a police dispatcher, wason her way to work at 2:24 p.m. when she heard what she thought was a blast."I heard an explosion, and i felt it in the South End," she said. "WhenI got here, they were getting calls from every place." 

An earthquake is not out of the questionin the St. Joseph area, which is part of the Nemaha Fault Zone that runsfrom Lincoln, Neb,. to Oklahoma City, said Edward Gray, earthquake programmanager for the Missouri Emergency Management Agency in Jefferson City.Twentyeight earthquakes of note have been documented from 1867 to 1961along the fault. 

"Most are buried rift systems, whichmeans they don't come to the surface," he said. "You can have a small earthquakearound that area." 

SPECIAL NOTE: 

The St. Joseph area specified in theAP article is not in the same time zone as Cincinnati, Missouri being inthe central time zone and Cincinnati being in the eastern. The boom wasexperienced in St. Joseph at 2:30 p.m., which would have been 3:30 p.m.in Cincinnati. 

On that day, Cincinnati had felt theblasts at 1:15 p.m., 2:00 p.m., 3:45 p.m. from Tate Township, 3:52 p.m.from Monroe Township, 3:55 p.m. from Washington Township, 4:00 p.m. fromMiami Township.
